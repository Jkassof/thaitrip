Wed, 12/12/18

Old Bangkok Walking Tour

Start the day with an early power breakfast in the hotel. From there we will take a hotel provided boat up the river to Chiang Pier where we will begin a 4km, 4-6 hour tour of old bangkok. We will hit the world famous Bangkok templates like Wat Pho (Temple of the Reclining Buddha) and Wat Phra Kaew (Temple of Jade Buddha) & the Grand Palace, as well as a number of other lesser known spots.


The bulk of Bangkok's must-see desitnations are in and around the former royal districtKo Ratanakosin. It's best to start early to beat both the heat and the hordes. Also ignore any strangers offering advice on sightseeing or shopping.

  • 1) Chiang Pier. Proceed east to the main gate of 2) Wat Phra Kaew & Grand Palace (p61), two of Bangkok's most famous attractions.
  • Backtrack, then head north along Th Maha Rat, and immediately afer passing the cat-laden newsstand (you'll know it when you smell it), turn left into 3) Trok Tha Wang, a narrow allewway holding a hidden classic Bangkok neighborhood. Turn left into crowded Trok Maha That to discover the cramped 4) amulet market (p68).
  • Exiting the market at Tha Phra Chan, cross Th Maha Rat and continue east until you reach 5) Sanam Luang, the "Royal Field."
  • Cross Th Ratchadamnoen Nai and go north, turning right at the Royal Hotel onto 6) Th Ratchadamnoen Klang, Bangkok's own Champs-Elysees
  • Continuing east, at the intersection with Th Tanae, you'll see the 7) October 14 memorial, commemorating the civiliation demonstrators who were killed on 14 October 1973 by the military during a pro-democracy rally. In the distance you'll see the 8) Democracy Monument, erected in 1932.
  • After a lunch stop at 9) Pok Spa Kar or 10) Chote Chitr, enter Th Phraeng Nara, crossing Khlong Lod, and continue west along Th Lak Meuang to the home of Bankok's city spirit, 11) Lak Meuang (p69).
  • Head south along the Sanam Chai and turn right onto Th Thai Wang, which will escourt you to the entrance of 12) Wat Pho (p61), home of the giant Reclining Buddha.

After that long afternoon of navigating the streets of old Bangkok, we will likely be pretty sweaty and gross. Time to head back to the hotel to clean up and get ready for a night out in Bangkok.

Evening

Street food rules in Chinatown, amking the area ideal for a colunary adventure. Although most vendors stay open late, the more popular stalls tend to sell out quickly so the best time to feast in this area is from 7pm to 9pm.

Start your walk at the intersection of Th Phaeng Nam and Th Charoen Krung. Head north along Th Phlap Phla Chai, staying on the right ahnd side for about 50m until you reach 1) Nai Mong Hoi Thod, a shophouse restaurant redowned for its delicious or soo an (oysters fried with egg and a stocky batter).

Backtrack to Th Charoen Kug and turn right. Upon reaching Th Mangkon make a right; on right left-hand side you'll see 2) Jek Phi, a table-less stall famous for its Chinese-style curries.

Cross Th Charoen Krug again, turn left, and continue easy until you reach Soi Yaowarat 6/Charoen Krung 16, also known as Talat Mai, the area's most famous strip of commerce. At the end of the alley you'll see a gentleman making 3) goo-ay dee-o koo-a gai, rice noodles fried with chicken, egg and garlic oil.

Upon emerging at Th Yaowarat, cross over to the bus market area directly across the street. The first vendor on the right, 4) Nay Lek Uan, sell goo-ay jap nam sai, an intensely peppery broth containing noodles and pork offal.

Returning to Th Yaowaray, turn right and conitinue until teh next intersection. On the corner of Th Yaowaphanit and Ta Yaowaray you'll see 5) Mangkorn Kao, a stall selling ba mee, chinese style wheat noodles, and barbecued pork.
